Genetic diversity in yams functions as insurance against future uncertainty—not a sentimental archive, but a live portfolio of options. When a single commercial cultivar dominates production, the loss of other varieties means the loss of traits for disease resistance, climate adaptation, and culinary diversity that may become critical tomorrow. The varieties that seem irrelevant today may be the ones that save next year's harvest. Preserving heirloom yams is not nostalgia—it is maintaining options. The following section examines what heirloom varieties are, why they matter across multiple dimensions, and how conservation efforts work in practice. 

### What is an "Heirloom" Yam?

An "heirloom" yam variety is one that has been grown for multiple generations and passed down through families. These varieties represent the historical legacy of yam cultivation and are a critical source of genetic diversity.

> **Key Information:** Heirloom yam varieties are those that have been grown for multiple generations and passed down through families. {{< link id="Y1D01" >}} Significant genetic diversity exists across species and landraces. {{< link id="Y7C01" >}}

Each heirloom variety represents a tested set of traits—flavor, texture, disease response, storage quality—that has already proven viable in real conditions. When commercial breeding needs resistance to a new disease or adaptation to a changing climate, those traits exist somewhere in the existing diversity. Losing a variety means losing an option that cannot be recovered.

Traditional varieties often have much more diversity in flavor and texture compared to modern commercial cultivars, which tend to be bred for uniformity and high yield.

> **Key Information:** Traditional varieties often have more diversity in flavor and texture than modern commercial cultivars. {{< link id="Y1D07" >}}

### Culturally and Regionally Significant Varieties

Many heirloom yams have significant cultural importance. For instance, *Dioscorea nummularia* is a traditionally cultivated variety in the Pacific Islands that holds great cultural value.

### Conservation Challenges

Preserving these varieties is not always easy. Some species, like the White Guinea yam (*Dioscorea rotundata*), are highly susceptible to yam mosaic virus, meaning they require virus-free planting material to remain viable.

> **Key Information:** White Guinea yam (*Dioscorea rotundata*) is highly susceptible to yam mosaic virus. {{< link id="Y1D06" >}}

To protect these genetic resources, researchers use a combination of techniques, including in-vitro conservation and germplasm banks, where genetic material is kept under controlled conditions.

> **Key Information:** In-vitro conservation and germplasm banks are used to preserve yam genetic resources. {{< link id="Y7C03" >}}

However, one of the most effective conservation methods remains on-farm conservation by traditional farmers. By continuing to grow these varieties in their own fields, farmers ensure that the diversity of the crop is maintained for future generations.

> **Key Information:** On-farm conservation by traditional farmers is a key approach to maintaining traditional yam landraces. {{< link id="Y7C08" >}}

This framing inverts the usual hierarchy between traditional and modern agriculture: the farmers maintaining these varieties on small plots are not preserving the past—they are actively maintaining the genetic options that commercial breeding depends on for future resilience. Commercial agriculture looks self-sufficient, but when conditions change, it goes back to the diversity that small-scale farmers kept alive. The archive is alive, and it is tended by people whose knowledge is irreplaceable. A germplasm bank can freeze tissue; it cannot freeze the knowledge of when to plant, how to store, or which variety handles which soil. Conservation that ignores farmer knowledge is an incomplete archive.
