# Yam Overview

Before we dive into the details of plant anatomy and history, let's start with a high-level overview of the true yam. The word "yam" is often used interchangeably with "sweet potato" in North America, but as you'll learn in the coming chapters, they're entirely different plants.

## What is a True Yam?

True yams belong to the genus *Dioscorea* and the family Dioscoreaceae. These are monocotyledonous plants, meaning they have a single seed leaf when they sprout. Most species produce large, starchy tubers that can reach lengths of over 1.5 meters (nearly 5 feet) and weigh as much as 70 kilograms (over 150 pounds).

Unlike many other tuberous plants, yams are climbing or trailing vines that require sturdy support to grow. Their heart-shaped leaves and unique branching patterns make them distinct from other common root crops.

## Key Characteristics

The true yam is a fascinating plant with several unique characteristics:

- **Tubers**: These are the starchy storage organs that grow underground.
- **Vines**: Yams are climbing or trailing vines that can reach lengths of over 10 meters (33 feet).
- **Leaves**: Many species have heart-shaped leaves with prominent veins.
- **Flowers**: Yam flowers are typically small and many species have separate male and female plants.

## Why it Matters

True yams are one of the most important staple crops in the world, particularly in West Africa, Southeast Asia, and the Pacific Islands. They provide a vital source of nutrition and are deeply ingrained in the cultural traditions of many societies. By understanding the biology, cultivation, and history of the true yam, you'll be well-prepared to explore the depth of its scientific and cultural significance.
