Yams succeed in desserts for three structural reasons: their starch holds when cooked, their mild flavor does not compete with sweeteners, and their color—whether purple or white—is stable through processing. The third reason is the most visually dramatic but the least understood: most natural food pigments degrade under heat or freezing; yam starch protects its color through a mechanism that surface-applied colorants cannot replicate. Color is not taxonomy; the Okinawan "beni imo" is a sweet potato, not a yam, despite sharing the same purple appearance.

### Starch and Structure

Before listing specific desserts, the foundational principle: yams work in desserts because their starch gelatinizes into a matrix that holds its shape under sugar concentration and fat exposure—properties that most other tubers do not share to the same degree.

The reason yams work so well in desserts is their unique chemistry.

> **Key Information:** Natural sweetness and an appropriate starch structure make certain yam varieties better suited for dessert applications. {{< link id="Y5C09" >}}

In Japan, this starchy property is used to make traditional confections like "Karukan."

> **Key Information:** Karukan is a Japanese confection that traditionally uses grated yam mixed with rice flour. {{< link id="Y5C05" >}}
