Up to half of all harvested yams rot before they can be eaten. For communities in West Africa — where over 90% of the world's yams grow — this loss is not a footnote. It is the central problem of yam agriculture.

Put differently: if West African harvests fail, there is no global backup supply.

> **Key Information:** Over 90% of global yam production comes from West Africa. {{< link id="Y6A02" >}}

Within West Africa, Nigeria produces more yams than any other country in the world.

> **Key Information:** Nigeria is the world's largest producer of yams. {{< link id="Y6A01" >}}

Because yams are so central to life in these areas, they have become an integral part of the local economy. In some traditional societies, they've even functioned as a way to measure wealth and prosperity.

> **Key Information:** In traditional West African societies, yams act as a staple food crop and sometimes a form of currency or wealth indicator. {{< link id="Y6A10" >}}

The Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) of the United Nations is the primary group that keeps track of how many yams are being grown and where they are going.

> **Key Information:** The Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) of the United Nations collects and publishes global yam production statistics. {{< link id="Y6A05" >}}

However, get a few yams out of the region and onto the global market is surprisingly difficult. Fresh tubers are heavy, full of water, and easily damaged.

> **Key Information:** Short shelf life and storage challenges most significantly limit international trade in fresh yams. {{< link id="Y6A03" >}}

These same challenges exist for local farmers as well. In some developing countries, up to half of all harvested yams may rot before they can ever be eaten or sold.

> **Key Information:** It is estimated that 30-60% of harvested yams are lost to spoilage in developing countries. {{< link id="Y6A09" >}}

For a farmer, these losses can be devastating, especially since market prices for yams can swing wildly depending on the harvest.

> **Key Information:** Weather conditions and resulting harvest yields most significantly affect year-to-year market prices for yams. {{< link id="Y6A08" >}}

Despite these risks, yams remain a critical crop for food security. Their biggest advantage is that, unlike many other tropical crops, they can be kept for months without any special equipment like refrigerators.

> **Key Information:** Yams are important for food security because they can be stored for several months without refrigeration. {{< link id="Y6B01" >}}

This makes them a perfect "hunger-gap" crop—providing a reliable source of food when other harvests have run out but the next ones aren't yet ready.

> **Key Information:** Yams contribute to household food security by providing storable food during seasonal hunger periods. {{< link id="Y6B03" >}}

For a subsistence farmer, though, expansion isn't always easy. Growing yams requires a huge amount of manual labor—digging mounds, staking vines, and harvesting deep tubers—and finding enough "seed" to plant the next crop is always a struggle.

> **Key Information:** Labor requirements and availability of planting material are the constraints that most limit yam production expansion by subsistence farmers. {{< link id="Y6B06" >}}

To get around the cost of buying new planting material every year, most farmers save some of their own crop to restart the next cycle.

> **Key Information:** Subsistence farmers maintain yam planting material by setting aside small tubers or pieces from their harvest. {{< link id="Y6B02" >}}

They also use smart agricultural techniques like intercropping—growing yams alongside other plants—and using many different yam varieties at once to reduce the risk of a single disease wiping out everything.

> **Key Information:** Traditional farmers mitigate risk by intercropping yams with other crops and growing multiple varieties. {{< link id="Y6B07" >}}

Even with these strategies, modern farmers are facing new challenges. Changes in rainfall and temperature are making it harder to know exactly when to plant or harvest.

> **Key Information:** Changing climate patterns are disrupting traditional planting and harvest timing for yams. {{< link id="Y6B05" >}}

There's also increased economic pressure. While yams are highly valued, cheaper and easier-to-grow alternatives like cassava and rice are winning in some regions — not because they're better, but because they're easier.

> **Key Information:** Competition from other starchy staples like cassava and rice is an economic trend affecting traditional yam production. {{< link id="Y6A06" >}}
