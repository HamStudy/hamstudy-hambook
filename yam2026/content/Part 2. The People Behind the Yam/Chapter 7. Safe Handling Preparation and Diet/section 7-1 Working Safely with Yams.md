A seatbelt does nothing on 999 drives. On the 1000th, it is the entire difference. The interventions in this section—gloves, acidulated water, extended boiling—are the seatbelts of yam preparation.

Before cooking begins, preparation handles four distinct risks: soil contamination, skin irritation from calcium oxalate crystals, quality loss from oxidation, and—in bitter varieties—toxin exposure. Each risk has a specific, simple intervention. The risks are manageable; they are not optional.

> **Key Information:** Peeling and cleaning is the first step in preparing most yam dishes. {{< link id="Y5A01" >}}

### Protecting Your Skin

Some varieties of yams contain calcium oxalate crystals. These needle-shaped structures physically puncture skin cells on contact, so the sting is not about having “sensitive” hands. Gloves are for all hands.

> **Key Information:** Gloves should be worn when peeling some varieties of yams because they may cause skin irritation due to calcium oxalate crystals. {{< link id="Y5A02" >}}

### Preventing Oxidation

Once you've peeled a yam, the flesh begins to react with the air. The wrong assumption is that a peeled yam can wait on the counter while you prepare other ingredients. It cannot. Enzymatic browning begins within seconds of the cut; by the time you set down the peeler, the clock is already running. This process, called oxidation, causes the yam to turn brown, much like a sliced apple. To keep your yams looking fresh and white (or purple), you should submerge them in water as soon as they are cut.

> **Key Information:** After peeling but before cooking, yams should be stored in cool water with lemon juice or vinegar to prevent browning. {{< link id="Y5A05" >}}

The acid slows the enzyme; it does not stop it. Acidulated water buys a short holding window, not indefinite storage.

### Handling Bitter Varieties

Some yam varieties are not merely unpleasant—they are toxic if not processed correctly. *Dioscorea dumetorum* contains compounds that require specific preparation to neutralize. The intervention is not optional; without it, the yam is unsafe to eat.

> **Key Information:** Bitter varieties of yam require special processing to remove toxic compounds and make them safe for consumption. {{< link id="Y4C02" >}}

> **Key Information:** A common precaution when preparing bitter varieties is extended boiling with multiple water changes to leach out the toxins. {{< link id="Y5A06" >}}

These compounds dissolve into the water more readily than heat alone can destroy them, so each fresh pot carries away another fraction of the toxin load.

### Cleaning the Skins

If you choose to cook yams with their skins on (common in some roasting methods), cleanliness is the priority.

> **Key Information:** When preparing yams with their skins, the skins should be thoroughly cleaned to remove soil contaminants. {{< link id="Y4C05" >}}
