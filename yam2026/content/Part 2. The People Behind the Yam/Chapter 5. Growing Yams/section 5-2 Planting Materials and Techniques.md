You don't plant yams with a packet of seeds. You plant yams with yams. The planting material you use, called "setts," are essentially cut-up pieces of the tuber itself. Getting this right is the difference between a farm full of empty mounds and a bumper harvest. Every sett represents a choice: this tuber could be eaten, or it could become next year's crop. The decision about which pieces become planting material and how they are prepared determines whether the farm reproduces itself or depletes its own inventory. A farm that consistently chooses correctly grows. A farm that consistently chooses poorly shrinks until it disappears. This is the reproductive economy of yam farming—the crop is the seed, the seed is the crop, and every planting decision is a bet on the future.

### Choosing Your Setts

**Stage 1 – Selection:** Not all pieces of a yam are created equal. A sett is like a battery pack: it must contain enough stored energy to fuel initial growth, and it must have a functional start switch in the form of viable buds. A piece with plenty of mass but no buds is inert—not a seed, a snack buried in the ground. A piece with buds but insufficient reserves will exhaust itself before the vine reaches sunlight. Both conditions produce empty mounds. The head or proximal portion of the tuber is preferred because it concentrates both assets: buds where growth originates, and mass where energy is stored. Choosing anything else is gambling with the farm's future.

Not all pieces of a yam are created equal. If you're a farmer looking to ensure your crop sprouts quickly, you'll go for the "head" or the proximal portion of the tuber.

> **Key Information:** The head (proximal) portion with buds is preferred for use as planting material. {{< link id="Y2B09" >}}

This is where the buds—the starting points for new vines—are most concentrated. You're looking for setts that show they're ready to grow.

> **Key Information:** The presence of viable buds or sprouts indicates that yam setts are suitable for planting. {{< link id="Y2B10" >}}

### Pre-Sprouting and Cleaning

**Stage 2 – Quality Control:** Many farmers don't just put their setts in the ground the day they cut them. They "pre-sprout" them first. This allows you to see which pieces are actually going to grow before you waste time and energy planting them in the field.

> **Key Information:** Pre-sprouting yam setts helps identify viable planting material and ensures uniform emergence. {{< link id="Y2B11" >}}

Before these pieces go in the dirt, you have to protect them. Freshly cut yam is a target for fungus and disease. To prevent this, you can dust or dip the setts.

> **Key Information:** Fungicide dusting or dipping is commonly applied to yam setts before planting to prevent rot and disease. {{< link id="Y2B03" >}}

For modern farmers, there are even cleaner ways to start.

> **Key Information:** **Tissue culture and minisett technology** are innovative techniques used to produce clean planting material for yams. {{< link id="Y2B06" >}}

### Putting Them in the Ground

**Stage 3 – Deployment:** When it's time to plant, the size of your setts matters. You're looking for pieces that are roughly 50 to 100 grams.

> **Key Information:** The recommended size for yam setts (pieces) used as planting material is **50-100 grams**. {{< link id="Y2B02" >}} Anything smaller might not have enough energy to get the vine to the surface, and anything larger is a waste of good food.

> **Key Information:** Tuber pieces (setts) are the primary planting material used for yam propagation. {{< link id="Y2B01" >}}

You'll want to plant these setts about 5 to 10 centimeters (2 to 4 inches) deep.

> **Key Information:** The optimal planting depth for yam setts is **5-10 cm (2-4 inches)**. {{< link id="Y2B05" >}}

In a traditional field, you'll space your mounds about 1 meter apart in each direction.

> **Key Information:** The recommended spacing between yam mounds or ridges in traditional cultivation is **1 meter by 1 meter**. {{< link id="Y2B07" >}} This gives each plant enough room to spread its roots and its leaves.

### Supporting the Vines

**Stage 4 – Infrastructure:** As the vines emerge, they need somewhere to go. Yams are naturally climbers, and they'll produce a much better harvest if they have a trellis or a stake to grow on. Staking is not vine management—it is yield engineering. A vine on the ground self-shades: only the top layer of leaves captures sunlight while the leaves underneath consume resources without contributing photosynthesis. A staked vine exposes every leaf to light, converting the plant's solar panel surface area from a thin strip to a wide curtain. More light means more sugar, and more sugar means a bigger tuber.

> **Key Information:** Stake or trellis systems for vine support are used to maximize yam yield in small spaces. {{< link id="Y2B08" >}}

Finally, some farmers use a technique called "milking." **Stage 5 – Partial Extraction:** Instead of harvesting the whole plant at once, they carefully dig up the large tubers mid-season, leaving the roots and vines intact. This allows the plant to grow a second, smaller harvest of seed yams for the next year.

> **Key Information:** "Milking" is the practice of harvesting tubers while leaving the root system intact for a second harvest. {{< link id="Y2B04" >}}
