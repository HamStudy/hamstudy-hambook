Harvesting yams is all about timing. The harvest window is a closing door: arrive too early and immature tubers cannot store—the season's investment evaporates in weeks. Arrive too late and fiber development begins while rot organisms establish themselves underground. The vine senescence signal is the plant's announcement that the door is open—it does not stay open. Each day of delay after maturity is not continued growth; it is accumulated risk that no amount of wishful thinking can reverse. The difference between food security and post-harvest loss is determined in the weeks before the harvest begins.

### Stage 1: Primary Signal — Vine Senescence

The most obvious sign that your yams are ready to come out of the ground is the vines. Yams don't just stop growing—the leaves and stems literally begin to die back. This process, known as senescence, is not a disease—it is the plant's final act. The yam is cannibalizing its own above-ground body, breaking down chlorophyll and shipping every remaining molecule of stored energy downward into the tuber. The yellowing you see is a directed demolition: the plant is dying on purpose so that you can eat. By the time the vines die back, the tuber has received everything the plant had left to give. That is why vine senescence is the primary indicator—the signal that the plant's growth cycle is complete and the stored energy is ready for harvest.

> **Key Information:** Senescence (yellowing and dying back) of the vines is the primary indicator that yams are ready for harvest. {{< link id="Y3A01" >}}

> **Key Information:** Senescence of the vines and drying of leaves is a visual indicator in the field that helps farmers determine yam harvest timing. {{< link id="Y3A08" >}}

### Stage 2: Supporting Signals — Calendar and Weather

For many varieties of white yam (*Dioscorea rotundata*), this process takes between 8 and 11 months.

> **Key Information:** Most varieties of Dioscorea rotundata (white yam) are typically harvested 8-11 months after planting. {{< link id="Y3A02" >}}

In West Africa, this usually means the big harvest happens between November and January.

> **Key Information:** In tropical West Africa, yams are typically harvested from November to January. {{< link id="Y3A04" >}}

However, other species take longer. Water yams (*Dioscorea alata*), for instance, often have a longer growing season than white yams.

> **Key Information:** Water yams (Dioscorea alata) can have a longer growing season than white yams. {{< link id="Y3A07" >}}

In traditional farming systems, the biggest factor that dictates when you harvest isn't the calendar or the moon; it's the weather.

> **Key Information:** Seasonal precipitation patterns most significantly affect the timing of yam harvests in traditional farming systems. {{< link id="Y3A06" >}}

### Stage 3: Verification — Checking the Tuber

If you're still not sure, you can always check. A common technique is to carefully dig away the soil from the top of the tuber to see its size and condition.

> **Key Information:** Carefully exposing the top of the tuber to check its size is a traditional technique used to determine if yams are mature enough for harvest. {{< link id="Y3A03" >}}

### Special Harvesting Systems

Some farmers use a two-stage harvest called "milking." This involves an early partial harvest, where the largest tubers are taken out while the vines and roots stay in the ground. This is followed by a second, final harvest later on.

> **Key Information:** Early partial harvest followed by a final harvest at full maturity is the timing practice used in the "milking" system of yam cultivation. {{< link id="Y3A09" >}}

Whatever system you use, do not delay once the plant is mature. At the point of readiness, every additional week in the ground is not continued growth—it is accumulating risk of fiber development and rot initiation. The harvest window does not stay open indefinitely. Think of post-maturity delay as an accumulation clock: first week, fiber threads begin forming in the outer cortex; second week, soil organisms find micro-cracks in the skin; third week, internal quality decline becomes visible on cut; fourth week, storage life is halved before the tuber even reaches the barn. None of these stages announce themselves above ground. The vines are already dead. The only signal was the one already received.

> **Key Information:** Yam tuber quality may become fibrous or begin to rot if harvesting is significantly delayed after maturity. {{< link id="Y3A05" >}}
