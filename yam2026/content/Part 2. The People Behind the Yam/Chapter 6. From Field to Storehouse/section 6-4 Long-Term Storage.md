A stored yam is not inert cargo. It is a living system running on its own reserves, and every condition you impose on it either extends its shelf life or accelerates its decline. The intuitive assumption that cooler and darker is always better fails here—yams are not potatoes, and the wrong temperature causes damage rather than preservation. The goal of long-term storage is not to halt biology but to slow it to the minimum sustainable rate: enough to keep the tuber alive and dormant without exhausting its starch reserves before the next planting season.

### The Ideal Conditions

The secret to long-term storage is to slow down the tuber's metabolism. If the temperature is too high, the yam will use up its energy and sprout. If it's too low, the tuber will rot.

> **Key Information:** The optimal temperature range for long-term yam storage is 59-64°F (15-18°C). {{< link id="Y3D02" >}}

This temperature range is significantly higher than the storage temperature for white potatoes, which can be stored much colder.

> **Key Information:** Proper ventilation is critical for long-term yam storage to prevent CO2 buildup and rot. {{< link id="Y3D05" >}}

High humidity is okay, but it must be paired with good airflow to prevent condensation and decay.

### The Typical Life Cycle

Properly cured and stored yams can last for a significant amount of time.

> **Key Information:** Properly cured and stored yams typically have a maximum storage duration of 4-6 months. {{< link id="Y3D04" >}}

The actual duration depends on the species. Some varieties have longer dormancy periods than others.

> **Key Information:** The dormancy period characteristic of each species is a key factor affecting the storage life of different yam varieties. {{< link id="Y3D10" >}}

Even while dormant, the tuber is still breathing.

> **Key Information:** Respiration and dormancy are physiological processes that continue during yam storage and affect storage duration. {{< link id="Y3D08" >}}

This respiration uses up the starch stored in the tuber, which is why it's so important to slow it down. A stored yam is a machine left idling: it consumes fuel even while it appears inactive. Temperature management is the throttle that controls how fast the engine runs.

### What to Avoid

Storage is a sensitive time. You have to be careful about what you store with your yams.

> **Key Information:** Yams should NOT be stored with fruits like apples and bananas because the ethylene produced by the fruits accelerates yam sprouting. {{< link id="Y3D03" >}}

You also have to be careful about light. Yams that are exposed to too much light will begin to turn green, which indicates the development of chlorophyll.

> **Key Information:** Exposure to light and the development of chlorophyll causes the green discoloration that sometimes develops in stored yams. {{< link id="Y3D07" >}}

### Defending the Harvest

Stored yams are a major target for rodents. To keep them safe, you can't just leave them on the floor.

> **Key Information:** Hanging individual tubers or using raised platforms with rat guards are storage techniques used to prevent rodent damage. {{< link id="Y3D06" >}}

For commercial storage, especially of seed yams, special facilities are needed.

> **Key Information:** Commercial long-term storage of seed yams uses temperature-controlled storage with careful monitoring. {{< link id="Y3D09" >}}

### The Yam Barn

In West Africa, the traditional structure for this is the "yam barn."

> **Key Information:** A yam barn with a thatched roof and open sides is the traditional yam storage structure used in West Africa. {{< link id="Y3D01" >}}

This simple design provides exactly what the yams need: shade, ventilation, and protection from the rain. The barn is not a historical artifact—it is a functional passive-climate system that solves the same problems a modern controlled-atmosphere storage solves, using airflow geometry instead of mechanical cooling.
