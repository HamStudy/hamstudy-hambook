A bruise made during extraction is not a minor blemish—it is an open entry point for infection that will manifest during storage as rot. Every cut or bruise sustained during harvest directly reduces the period the tuber can be kept edible. This means the quality of the harvest operation determines how long the harvest lasts. Physical work yes; celebration second; delicacy first.

### The Right Tools

In smallholder farms around the world, you won't see heavy machinery in the yam field. Instead, you'll see a simple but effective set of tools.

> **Key Information:** A wooden digging stick or hoe is a traditional tool commonly used for harvesting yams in smallholder farming systems. {{< link id="Y3B01" >}}

These tools allow the farmer to feel their way around the tuber. This is especially important for species like *Dioscorea rotundata*, where the tubers can grow more than a meter deep into the soil.

> **Key Information:** Certain yam species are particularly challenging to harvest due to their depth and potential length, with some growing more than 1 meter deep. {{< link id="Y3B04" >}}

### Safe Extraction

The goal of a safe harvest is to remove the tuber without any cuts or bruises. The intuitive move is to pull on the vine. The correct move is to dig away the soil until the tuber is free. Pulling without loosening the soil first drags the tuber against resistance, creating internal fractures and skin damage that are invisible at harvest but fatal during storage.

> **Key Information:** Carefully removing soil from around the tuber before lifting is a technique that helps minimize damage to yam tubers during harvesting. {{< link id="Y3B02" >}}

This is even more critical in heavy clay soils, where the dirt can grip the tuber and cause it to break.

> **Key Information:** Loosening soil carefully to avoid breaking the tubers is recommended when harvesting yams in heavy clay soils. {{< link id="Y3B05" >}}

Every cut or bruise is an open door for infection and rot. That's why avoiding damage is the number one priority.

> **Key Information:** Care is taken to avoid cuts and bruises when harvesting yams to prevent infection and reduce storage losses. {{< link id="Y3B03" >}}

Do not yank what you intend to store.

### Post-Harvest Field Work

What you do with the rest of the plant also matters. A harvest that leaves vines and tubers in place is an incomplete harvest—the field remains a habitat for the organisms that damage the next crop. The vines shouldn't be left in the field where they can harbor pests. Instead, they should be removed and used for something else.

> **Key Information:** Yam vines during harvest should be removed and used for mulch or compost. {{< link id="Y3B06" >}}

Once the field is clear, you shouldn't just plant yams in the same spots. To keep the soil healthy and the pest levels low, you need to rotate your crops.

> **Key Information:** Planting a rotation crop or cover crop is a post-harvest field management practice recommended in yam cultivation systems. {{< link id="Y3B08" >}}

### Modern Harvesting

While traditional methods are still the most common, mechanized harvesting is possible.

> **Key Information:** Mechanized yam harvesting uses adapted diggers that lift and expose tubers for collection, saving manual labor on larger farms. {{< link id="Y3B07" >}}
